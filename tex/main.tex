    This study evaluated the performance of Newton-Raphson and Halley's method in solving eccentric anomaly in Keppler's first law. Since the equation is a transcendental equation, solving it algebraically is impossible and it requires numerical methods to solve. This study involves the comparison of both methods and determine whether Halley's method outperforms Newton-Raphson method not only in convergence rate but in other criteria such as execution time. Using python programming language and relevant packages, both methods were implemented and tested with four different starting values. The convergence rate, average execution time, and standard deviation was recorded. Simple Additive Weighting (SAW) was used to score the algorithms in the mentioned metrics with equal weights. The results showed that Halley's method outperformed Newton-Raphson method not only in convergence rate but also in average execution time regardless of it's complexity. The study also highlighted the significance of starting values for numerical methods, in particular, to finding the eccentric anomaly in Kepler's first law. The findings of this study contribute to the understanding of numerical methods for solving real world problems that involve transcendental equations.

\section{Preliminaries}

\subsection{Orbital Mechanics and Kepler's Equation}

Orbital mechanics is the study of the motion of celestial objects under the influence of gravitational force. The motion of the planets and satellites is generally explained by Kepler's laws of planetary motion. Kepler's laws were derived by Johannes Kepler based on the observations of Tycho Brahe. According to Kepler's first law, the path of the motion of the planets is elliptical with the sun at one of the focuses. The second law of Kepler's states that the line joining the sun to the planet sweeps out an equal area in an equal time. The third law of Kepler's establishes a relationship between the period of the orbit and the semi-major axis of the orbit, expressed as $T^2 \propto a^3$. These principles form the foundation of classical orbital mechanics and are widely used in astronomy, astrophysics, and satellite motion analysis \parencite{salah2025kepler}.

To determine the position of a body in an elliptical orbit at a specified time, the field of orbital mechanics has proposed angular parameters called anomalies, which include the mean anomaly, eccentric anomaly, and true anomaly. The relationship between the mean anomaly $M$, eccentric anomaly $E$, and orbital eccentricity $e$ is given by Kepler's Equation

\begin{equation*}
M = E - e \sin E
\end{equation*}

\noindent where $0 \leq e \le 1$ represents the eccentricity of the orbit.

The equation is a transcendental equation because the variable E appears both algebraically and trigonometrically. This means that the equation cannot be solved algebraically, but numerical methods must be used to find the value of the eccentric anomaly E. This is done by rearranging the equation
\begin{equation*}
    E - e \sin E - M = 0
\end{equation*}
In this regard, the problem becomes a nonlinear root-finding problem that can be addressed using various iterative numerical methods such as fixed-point iteration, Newton's method, and Halley's method. These numerical methods can be employed in orbital computations for obtaining accurate approximations for the eccentric anomaly \parencite{salah2025kepler}.
 
\subsection{Numerical Root-Finding Algorithms}

\subsubsection{Newton-Raphson Method}

The Newton-Raphson method, often simply called Newton's method, has its origins in the late 17th century. Isaac Newton first described a rudimentary version of the method in 1669, strictly applying it to polynomial equations by substituting successive approximations. In 1690, Joseph Raphson published a simplified, generalized version of Newton's process that abstracted the method into the iterative sequence we recognize today, though still primarily for polynomials. It was not until 1740 that Thomas Simpson applied formalized calculus and derivatives to extend the method to non-linear and transcendental functions, finalizing the modern formula \parencite{ypma1995historical}. Mathematically, the method relies on approximating a function near a point pn by its tangent line and using the x-intercept of the tangent as the next approximation. This process uses local linearization based on the first-order Taylor series expansion \parencite{burden2016numerical}. If pn is close to the true root p, setting the first-order Taylor expansion equal to zero yields the Newton iteration formula for the next approximation:

\begin{equation*}
    p_{n+1} = p_n - \frac{f(p_n)}{f'(p_n)}
\end{equation*}

A major advantage of Newton's method is its rate of convergence. If $f(p) = 0$ and $f'(p) \neq 0 $(meaning p is a simple root), the method converges at least quadratically for an initial guess $p0$ that is sufficiently close to p \parencite{burden2016numerical}. However, if the root has a multiplicity greater than 1, meaning $f'(p) = 0$, the method typically loses its quadratic convergence and slows down to a linear rate, which may cause it to behave poorly. Additionally, because the method requires the evaluation of both the function $f(x)$ and its first derivative $f'(x)$ at every step, it can be computationally expensive or difficult if the derivative is complex to isolate \parencite{burden2016numerical}.



\subsubsection{Halley's Method}

Halley's method is a root-finding algorithm that serves as a higher-order extension of Newton's method. It was introduced in 1694 by the English astronomer and mathematician Edmond Halley, who is most famous for computing the orbit of the eponymous Halley's Comet \parencite{scavo1995geometry}. Working extensively with orbital mechanics, Halley sought a method that converged faster than what Newton and Raphson had established, specifically to solve complex astronomical calculations like Kepler's equation. While the Newton-Raphson method relies on a linear approximation using the first derivative, Halley's method uses a quadratic approximation of the function \parencite{scavo1995geometry}. It incorporates the second derivative to account for the curvature of the function, which allows the iterative steps to approximate the non-linear behavior of the function much more accurately than a straight tangent line. The iterative formula for Halley's method incorporates the second derivative $f''(pn)$ and is expressed as:

\begin{equation*}
    p_{n+1} = p_n - \dfrac{2f(p_n) f'(p_n)}{2[f'(p_n)]^2 - f(p_n) f''(p_n)}
\end{equation*}

Because it utilizes up to the second derivative, Halley's method is more computationally expensive per iteration than Newton's method. However, it achieves cubic convergence for simple roots \parencite{burden2016numerical}. This means that, under optimal conditions, the number of correct digits roughly triples with each iteration, often resulting in fewer total iterations required to reach a highly accurate root, making it highly stable even under difficult initial conditions.


\subsection{ Multi-Criteria Decision Making \& Simple Additive Weighting}

Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) refers to a mathematical framework used to evaluate and choose among multiple alternatives based on several, often conflicting, criteria. In computational and algorithmic analysis, no single performance metric dictates the absolute superiority of a method. An algorithm might converge in very few iterations but require immense computational power per step. MCDM provides a structured approach to balance these various performance metrics, such as convergence rate, mathematical efficiency, and runtime stability to determine the most optimal algorithm overall.

One of the most straightforward and widely implemented MCDM techniques is Simple Additive Weighting (SAW), sometimes referred to as the weighted sum method \parencite{tzeng2011multiple}. The SAW method evaluates alternatives by calculating a cumulative weighted score for each option. The process involves two primary steps: data normalization and weighted evaluation. Because different criteria often have completely different units and scales, such as the discrete number of iterations versus execution time measured in microseconds the raw empirical data must first be transformed into a comparable, dimensionless scale. For criteria where a lower value is preferable, which are categorized as cost criteria (e.g., lower iteration count, lower execution time, and lower standard deviation), the normalization is typically calculated as:
\begin{equation*}
    r_{ij} = \dfrac{min_i(x_{ij})}{x_{ij}}
\end{equation*}

where xij is the raw performance value of alternative $i$ for criterion $j$, $min_i(x_{ij})$ is the best performance value among all alternatives for that criterion, and rij is the resulting normalized value. Once all data points are normalized, the overall SAW score Si for each alternative is computed by multiplying the normalized values by their respective predefined weights wj and summing the results:

\begin{equation*}
    S_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} w_j r_{ij}
\end{equation*}

In this formulation, the assigned weights must sum to exactly 1, representing the proportional priority of each criterion. The algorithm with the highest final score Si is mathematically considered the most optimal choice, as it represents the best overall balance of the evaluated criteria \parencite{tzeng2011multiple}. By utilizing SAW, a holistic and objective ranking can be established between Newton-Raphson and Halley's methods without biasing the conclusion toward one specific performance metric.


\section{Methods and Applications}

The Keppler's First law relates the mean anomaly, M, and the eccentric anomaly, E, as follows:
\begin{equation*}
M = E - e \sin E
\end{equation*}

With that, the function to approximately find the root with the numerical methods is as follows:
	\begin{equation*}
        f(E) = E - e \sin E - M
    \end{equation*}
Where $E$ is the eccentric anomaly, $M$ is the mean anomaly, and $e$ is the eccentricity of the orbit.

\subsection{Newton Raphson Method}
The first derivative of the function f(E) is needed by the Newton-Raphson method and was calculated as follows:
\begin{equation*}
f'(E) = 1 - e \cos(E)
\end{equation*}
The pseudo code of the Newton-Raphson method is as follows:

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Newton-Raphson Method}
\label{alg:newton_raphson}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require $f, f', x_0, tol, max\_iter$
    \Ensure Root approximation and iteration count
    
    \State $x_{current} \gets x_0$
    \State $iter\_count \gets 0$
    \State $\epsilon \gets 10^{-10}$ \Comment{Small value to prevent division by zero}

    \While{$iter\_count < max\_iter$}
        \State $fx \gets f(x_{current})$
        \State $dfx \gets f'(x_{current})$

        \If{$|dfx| < \epsilon$}
            \State \textbf{print} ``Derivative too small; possible divergence.''
            \State \Return \textbf{None}
        \EndIf

        \State $x_{next} \gets x_{current} - \frac{fx}{dfx}$ \Comment{Newton-Raphson Update}

        \If{$|x_{next} - x_{current}| < tol$}
            \State \Return $x_{next}, iter\_count$
        \EndIf

        \State $x_{current} \gets x_{next}$
        \State $iter\_count \gets iter\_count + 1$
    \EndWhile

    \State \Return $x_{current}, iter\_count$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

This code follows the exact steps of the Newton-Raphson algorithm. First, the algorithm takes in the function $f$, its derivative $f'$, the initial guess $x_0$, the error tolerance $tol$, and the maximum allowed iterations $max\_iter$. It sets our current guess ($x_{current}$) to $x_0$ and the iteration count to $0$, then starts a \texttt{while} loop. 

Inside the loop, it evaluates the function ($f_x$) and its derivative ($f'_x$) at the current guess. It includes a safety check to see if the derivative is too small ($|f'_x| < \epsilon$), returning a divergence warning and stopping if it risks dividing by zero. Next, it calculates the new guess ($x_{next}$) by subtracting the ratio of $f_x$ over $f'_x$ from the current guess:

\[ x_{next} = x_{current} - \frac{f(x_{current})}{f'(x_{current})} \]

If the absolute difference between the new and old guess is smaller than our tolerance ($tol$), it successfully stops and returns the root. If not, it updates the current guess to $x_{next}$, adds one to the iteration count, and repeats the process until it converges or finishes the loop.

\subsection{Halley's Method}

	As for Halley's method, the second derivative of the function f(E) is required to compute the root. The second derivative f''(E) is as follows:
\begin{equation*}
f''(E) = e \sin(E)
\end{equation*}
The pseudo code of Halley's method is as follows


\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Halley's Method}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require $f, f', f'', x_0, tol, max\_iter$
    \Ensure Root approximation and iteration count
    
    \State $x_{current} \gets x_0$
    \State $iter\_count \gets 0$

    \While{$iter\_count < max\_iter$}
        \State $fx \gets f(x_{current})$
        \State $dfx \gets f'(x_{current})$
        \State $ddfx \gets f''(x_{current})$

        \State $num \gets 2 \cdot fx \cdot dfx$
        \State $den \gets 2 \cdot (dfx)^2 - fx \cdot ddfx$
        
        \State $x_{next} \gets x_{current} - \frac{num}{den}$

        \If{$|x_{next} - x_{current}| < tol$}
            \State \Return $x_{next}, iter\_count$
        \EndIf

        \State $x_{current} \gets x_{next}$
        \State $iter\_count \gets iter\_count + 1$
    \EndWhile

    \State \Return $x_{current}, iter\_count$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

For Halley's method, the algorithm structure is nearly identical to the Newton-Raphson algorithm but requires the second derivative ($f''$) as an additional input. It sets up the same starting values and enters the \texttt{while} loop. Inside the loop, it evaluates the function ($f_x$), the first derivative ($f'_x$), and the second derivative ($f''_x$). Instead of a simple ratio, the update step calculates the next guess ($x_{next}$) using the following second-order formula:

\[ x_{next} = x_{current} - \frac{2 f(x_{current}) f'(x_{current})}{2 [f'(x_{current})]^2 - f(x_{current}) f''(x_{current})} \]

Just like the first method, it checks if the absolute difference between $x_{next}$ and $x_{current}$ is less than the tolerance ($tol$). If the condition is met, it returns the newly found root and iteration count; otherwise, it updates the current guess to $x_{next}$, increases the iteration count, and continues looping.

\section{Conclusion}

In conclusion, the study evaluated the performance of two numerical root finding methods, the Newton-Raphson and Halley's numerical methods in solving Kepler's Equation for high-eccentricity orbits with e = 0.999. Upon analyzing four
 distinct test cases with varying initial conditions ($E0$) as adapted from the study of \textcite{ozuomba2014comparative}, the study was able to identify the critical trade-offs between mathematical convergence and computational execution.

The initial value, $E_0$, was found to have significant influence on the convergence for both methods. As the test case progressed from simple initial guess to a calculated and complex guess, the iterations  needed for both methods to converge decreased significantly.

The findings of the study indicate that Halley's method is better in terms of mathematical convergence, requiring fewer iterations compared to the Newton-Raphson method to find a solution.  Newton-Raphson method also struggles with poor initial guess, as evident in test case 1, where it took 44 iterations to converge to a solution. On the other hand, the Newton-Raphson method showed higher computational efficiency in cases with better initial approximations and consistently achieving faster average runtimes (as low as 2.18 µs) most likely due to its lower algorithmic complexity compared to Halley's method which requires the first and the second derivatives of the function.

Upon analysis using Simple Additive Weighting (SAW), where the criteria were iteration count, average runtime, and stability (standard deviation), a ranking that favored Halley's method was found. With a mean SAW score of $0.57$ against Newton-Raphson's $0.36$, Halley's method was shown to be a more reliable solver for solving the eccentric anomaly in Keppler's equation. While Newton-Raphson is little bit faster in some scenarios, Halley's method's is able to maintain rapid convergence under difficult initial conditions makes it the more effective choice.

\section{Recommendations}

Based on the results and conclusions of this study, the following are recommended for future research and practical application:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Low level implementations}. The python programming language was used in the study which is a high-level language. Future research should implement these algorithms in low-level languages such as C++ or Assembly. This would allow for a better assessment for both methods. 
    \item \textbf{Memory as metric.} It is recommended to also evaluate the memory as a potential criteria to see how much memory each numerical method consumes which may be crucial in some low-resource devices.
    \item \textbf{Expansion of Decision Criteria} It is recommended to change the weights of the SAW criteria based on specific profiles. For example, a higher weight in average runtime may be needed for comparison in minimizing power consumption of these numerical methods.


\end{enumerate}

\begin{Verbatim}[

from typing import Callable, Optional, Tuple

def newtons_method(
    f: Callable[[float], float], 
    df: Callable[[float], float], 
    p0: float, 
    tol: float=1e-5, 
    n0: int=100,
) -> Optional[Tuple[float, int]]:
    i: int = 1
    while i <= n0:
        p: float = p0 - f(p0) / df(p0)
        abs_error = abs(p - p0)
        
        if abs_error < tol:
            return (p, i)
        
        i += 1
        p0 = p
        
    print("Newton did not converge after", n0, "iterations.")
    return None
\end{Verbatim}

\subsubsection{Halley's Method}
\begin{Verbatim}[frame=single, framesep=10pt, label=Python: Halley's Method]
def solve_halley(f, df, ddf, E_n, tol=1e-5, iter=100):
    cycles = 0
    for i in range(iter):
        cycles += 1
        f_val = f(E_n)
        df_val = df(E_n)
        ddf_val = ddf(E_n)
        
        # Halley's Step Formula
        numerator = 2 * f_val * df_val
        denominator = 2 * (df_val**2) - f_val * ddf_val
        correction = numerator / denominator
        
        E_next = E_n - correction
        
        if abs(E_next - E_n) < tol:
            return E_next, cycles
        E_n = E_next
    return None
\end{Verbatim}
